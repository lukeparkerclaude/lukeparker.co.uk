Taking Action: Why Our Council's Migration Emergency Declaration Matters

As a Reform UK councillor, I've witnessed firsthand how our current immigration system fails both British communities and legitimate migrants alike. That's why I'm proud to support our council's decision to declare a migration emergency – a move that reflects the real concerns of residents who feel their voices have been ignored by Westminster for far too long.

This declaration isn't about political posturing; it's about accountability and honest governance. For years, successive governments have implemented immigration policies without proper consultation with local authorities who bear the practical consequences. We're talking about housing shortages, strained public services, and communities struggling to cope with rapid demographic changes – all while central government maintains a hands-off approach to the real-world impact of their decisions.

The Data Speaks for Itself

The numbers don't lie. Our local housing waiting lists have grown by 40% in the past three years, while NHS dental appointments remain unavailable for months. School places are oversubscribed, and our social services are stretched beyond capacity. When I raise these concerns, I'm not fear-mongering – I'm presenting evidence-based observations that any competent government should address proactively.

What frustrates me most is the waste inherent in our current system. We spend billions on an immigration apparatus that fails to distinguish between genuine asylum seekers and economic migrants, while simultaneously failing to provide adequate support for integration. This isn't just inefficient; it's actively harmful to social cohesion.

Reform UK has consistently argued for an Australian-style points-based system that prioritises skills and genuine need. Such an approach would eliminate much of the current chaos while ensuring we attract migrants who can contribute meaningfully to British society. Instead, we have a system that pleases nobody – not British communities, not genuine refugees, and certainly not the taxpayers footing the bill.

Local Democracy in Action

Our migration emergency declaration demonstrates something crucial: local democracy still matters. While Westminster politicians make grand pronouncements from their ivory towers, it's councillors like myself who must deal with the practical consequences of their policies. We see the overcrowded GP surgeries, the housing pressure, and the community tensions that arise when change happens too rapidly without proper planning.

This declaration sends a clear message to central government: you cannot continue implementing policies without considering their local impact. Every decision has consequences, and those consequences are felt most acutely in communities like ours, where resources are already stretched thin.

I believe in controlled, managed immigration that serves Britain's interests while maintaining our humanitarian obligations. What we have now is neither controlled nor managed – it's a system running on autopilot while communities struggle to adapt.

A Call for Systemic Reform

This emergency declaration is just the beginning. We need fundamental reform of how immigration policy is developed and implemented. Local authorities should have meaningful input into decisions that affect their communities. We need honest conversations about capacity, integration, and the true costs of current policies.

Reform UK stands for efficient, accountable government that serves British interests first. That doesn't make us anti-immigrant; it makes us pro-British community. There's a world of difference between the two, though our political opponents would have you believe otherwise.

Moving forward, I'll continue pushing for transparency in immigration data, proper consultation with local communities, and policies that prioritise integration over simply processing numbers. Our residents deserve better than the current chaos, and I'm committed to ensuring their voices are heard in Westminster.

The time for platitudes is over. We need action, accountability, and genuine reform of our broken immigration system.
